Meaningful Controller (Sit’n Spin)

Interaction Design / Electronics / UI Design

CLIENT:

Personal Project

Date

Spring 2015

Team

Donald Hoffer, Andrew Ames and Lynne Moidel

Project Description

The original project was to design and build a meaningful controller. I choose to repurpose a childhood toy, the sit’n spin, and through the use of electronics I enhanced the play experience and gave the user interactive feedback.

Concept

The sit’n spin will work as the controller and will be the physical interface for a two player collaborative game. The concept is to work together and test one’s ability to follow directions.

Prototype measurements (part 1)

Rules

One player communicates to the other which direction to spin and how fast to go. The computer monitors the rate of the spin and the direction to determine success. If the players follow the directions correctly they will move to the next level.

Process

We first built two maquettes and then one working prototype in order to plan for how the controller will work. The electronics were then incorporated into the device to be able to measure the speed.

Program Design

The player will spin until they reach a certain speed that enters the hitbox. Once reached, the countdown will start, and the player will need to keep his/her speed constant until the countdown finishes.

Program design

Potential for Use with the Treatment of Autism

Many children and adults with Autism have challenges in processing sensory information. One option for treatment is called Occupational Therapy. During a session, the therapist and patient will interact in a sensory-rich environment containing activities to help the patient develop automatic and appropriate responses to sensation.

My device would work well for this type of treatment because it provides a unique and challenging experience for the patient. With additional studies and research into Occupational Therapy, I hope to turn my project into a tool to help children and adults who have Autism process sensory information.
